# Accessible Genius Methods

These cards distill durable methods from historical and modern high-output
thinkers into accessible agent skills. Use them as process primitives, not as
claims that a model can imitate a person.

## Selection Rule

Admit a method only when it survives modern limiters:

- It can be stated as an artifact, checklist, loop, or validation gate.
- It works without charisma, coercion, secrecy, or extreme personal sacrifice.
- It has a falsifiable or reviewable output.
- It improves safety, quality, discovery, clarity, speed, or expressiveness.
- Its misuse conditions can be named.

Reject or downgrade:

- cult-of-genius imitation,
- "move fast and break things" without rollback/stable infrastructure,
- blitzscaling without a winner-take-most speed thesis and financing runway,
- first-principles rhetoric that ignores empirical constraints,
- founder-mode or wartime-mode excuses for abusive or opaque decisions,
- productivity myths based only on lifestyle anecdotes,
- investment methods that become personalized buy/sell advice, performance
  guarantees, nonpublic-information use, or leverage without risk limits.

## Long-list Candidate Backlog

This backlog is intentionally broader than the active method cards. It keeps
candidate lineages visible without activating them by default. Promote a
candidate only after extracting a durable artifact, limiter, and validation
step.

Use the backlog to avoid regional, disciplinary, or Silicon Valley recency
bias. Do not cite a candidate as evidence until the relevant source has been
checked and recorded.

## Method Cards

### 1. Dialectic Refutation Gate

Lineage: Socrates, Nyaya, Nagarjuna, Popper.

Use when:
- the answer may rest on hidden assumptions;
- legal, scientific, benchmark, security, or product claims need stress;
- the user brought a persuasive but unverified draft.

Artifact:

```text
claim:
hidden premises:
strongest objection:
counterexample:
test that could falsify it:
surviving claim:
confidence:
```

Validation:
- At least one credible counterexample or failure mode is considered.
- The final claim is narrower than the initial claim when evidence requires it.

Do not use when:
- the user only needs a direct mechanical action and the premises are already
  explicit.

### 2. Formal Contract and Grammar Gate

Lineage: Euclid, Panini, Leibniz, Frege, Hilbert, Turing, Shannon.

Use when:
- a task needs strict output shape, APIs, types, grammar, schemas, or proofs;
- a repo change may break callers;
- a benchmark answer needs an exact contract.

Artifact:

```text
primitive terms:
allowed operations:
forbidden operations:
input contract:
output contract:
invariants:
edge cases:
equivalence / non-equivalence:
```

Validation:
- Every later action can be traced to an allowed operation or named exception.
- Contract changes list call sites, compatibility wrapper, or migration path.

Do not use when:
- it would freeze an exploratory or creative task before the shape is known.

### 3. Empirical Experiment Ledger

Lineage: Ibn al-Haytham, Galileo, Francis Bacon, Lavoisier.

Use when:
- a conclusion can be checked against observation, measurements, logs, tests,
  screenshots, or user behavior;
- a claim risks sounding plausible without evidence.

Artifact:

```text
hypothesis:
observable:
instrument/tool:
control or baseline:
procedure:
result:
confounders:
next experiment:
```

Validation:
- Observation is separated from interpretation.
- A negative result changes the plan instead of being explained away.

Do not use when:
- the task is purely normative or preference-based; use deliberation instead.

### 4. Evidence Garden and Gap Prediction

Lineage: Darwin, Linnaeus, Mendeleev, Florence Nightingale.

Use when:
- many weak signals must accumulate into a pattern;
- gaps, unknown categories, or missing cases matter;
- the output should persuade through organized evidence.

Artifact:

```text
taxonomy:
evidence rows:
outliers:
missing cells:
predicted missing item:
evidence that would confirm:
evidence that would refute:
visual/table form:
```

Validation:
- Missing cells are explicit, not silently filled.
- Predictions are useful enough to guide collection or tests.

Do not use when:
- there are too few observations to justify a taxonomy.

### 5. Thought Experiment Simplifier

Lineage: Einstein, Maxwell, Feynman, Polya.

Use when:
- a complex system needs a minimal mental model;
- the answer is stuck in jargon;
- a mechanism, bug, physics-like tradeoff, or algorithm needs explanation.

Artifact:

```text
toy world:
entities:
rules:
extreme cases:
what must remain true:
where toy model breaks:
next real-world check:
```

Validation:
- The toy model predicts at least one real test or edge case.
- Simplification does not erase safety, legal, or user constraints.

Do not use when:
- exact domain rules are already available and simplification would introduce
  false analogies.

### 6. Gemba Kaizen Validation

Lineage: Toyota Production System, Taiichi Ohno, W. Edwards Deming.

Use when:
- a workflow, UI, production bug, CI failure, or ops problem depends on real
  execution;
- second-hand summaries conflict with the actual system.

Artifact:

```text
real place / system:
current flow:
waste / delay / defect:
frontline evidence:
smallest reversible improvement:
measure:
standardize or revert:
```

Validation:
- The agent inspects the real artifact, log, screen, command, or workflow.
- Improvement is small enough to validate and revert.

Do not use when:
- access would require secrets, private data, or unsafe production mutations
  outside the user's authorization.

### 7. Fog and Friction Strategy Loop

Lineage: Sun Tzu, Kautilya, Clausewitz, John Boyd.

Use when:
- information is incomplete, adversarial, delayed, or unreliable;
- defensive security, CI incident response, migrations, or competitive product
  decisions need sequencing.

Artifact:

```text
terrain:
actors:
knowns / unknowns:
friction:
fog:
observe:
orient:
decide:
act:
rollback / reserve:
```

Validation:
- The plan names uncertainty instead of pretending it is gone.
- Actions are staged so new observations can change the next move.

Do not use when:
- it would turn ordinary collaboration into adversarial framing.

### 8. Pattern Language Composer

Lineage: Vitruvius, Brunelleschi, Christopher Alexander, Dieter Rams.

Use when:
- UI, architecture, documents, spatial systems, or skills need reusable design;
- repeated local solutions are becoming inconsistent.

Artifact:

```text
recurring problem:
context:
forces:
pattern:
implementation sketch:
neighboring patterns:
misuse:
example:
```

Validation:
- Pattern solves a recurring problem and names where it should not be used.
- It composes with neighboring patterns instead of creating an isolated rule.

Do not use when:
- a one-off fix is safer than creating a reusable abstraction.

### 9. Narrative Resonance Harness

Lineage: Aristotle, Murasaki Shikibu, Shakespeare, Basho, Dostoevsky, Le Guin.

Use when:
- prose, UX copy, game/UI affect, explanation, or presentation needs emotional
  force without over-explaining;
- the task needs rhythm, viewpoint, tension, and aftertaste.

Artifact:

```text
audience:
viewpoint:
desire:
obstacle:
turning image:
motifs:
voice constraints:
ending aftertaste:
avoid-list:
reader validation:
```

Validation:
- Every paragraph or screen advances pressure, image, information, or choice.
- Emotional labels are replaced with observable detail where appropriate.

Do not use when:
- the user needs dry contractual, safety, or incident language.

### 10. Customer-Backwards Mechanism

Lineage: Steve Jobs, Amazon working backwards, AWS PR/FAQ discipline.

Use when:
- a product, feature, doc, or tool must be judged from the user's end state;
- teams are debating implementation before value is clear.

Artifact:

```text
customer/user:
pain:
press-release promise:
FAQ objections:
non-goals:
success metric:
experience walkthrough:
implementation implications:
```

Validation:
- The promise is understandable before the implementation details.
- FAQ covers adoption, trust, failure, pricing/cost, support, and rollback.

Do not use when:
- the task is internal infrastructure with no meaningful user-facing promise;
  use contract and gemba gates instead.

### 11. Unscalable Truth Probe

Lineage: Paul Graham, Y Combinator, early startup field practice.

Use when:
- the system needs real user feedback before automation or scaling;
- assumptions about demand, usability, or value are untested.

Artifact:

```text
manual action:
target user:
expected signal:
direct observation:
what surprised us:
automation later:
scale blocker:
kill / continue threshold:
```

Validation:
- The probe creates contact with real behavior, not vanity metrics.
- Automation waits until the manual path teaches something.

Do not use when:
- manual handling would violate privacy, safety, or fairness expectations.

### 12. First-Principles Moonshot Gate

Lineage: Larry Page, Google X, Elon Musk, Jensen Huang.

Use when:
- incremental assumptions block a large gain;
- materials, constraints, cost drivers, or hardware/software bottlenecks can be
  decomposed;
- a 10x target may be clearer than a 10 percent improvement.

Artifact:

```text
target leap:
current assumption:
first-principles decomposition:
hard constraints:
false constraints:
10x path:
feasibility evidence:
kill criteria:
```

Validation:
- The decomposition names physical, legal, cost, supply, compute, and human
  constraints separately.
- The moonshot has a kill criterion and an evidence-gathering path.

Do not use when:
- the user needs a low-risk maintenance fix, regulatory compliance, or support
  response where reliability beats ambition.

### 13. Transparent Flat Technical Review

Lineage: Jensen Huang, engineering-led flat review cultures.

Use when:
- complex technical decisions need shared context across specialists;
- private summaries are causing information bottlenecks.

Artifact:

```text
decision:
specialists needed:
shared briefing:
open questions:
risks:
decision owner:
decision log:
who must be informed:
```

Validation:
- Important reasoning is shareable by default unless confidentiality blocks it.
- Decision rights remain explicit; flat review is not consensus paralysis.

Do not use when:
- privacy, HR, legal, security, or vulnerable-person issues require restricted
  distribution.

### 14. Stable-Speed Delivery Gate

Lineage: Meta's post-breakage "move fast with stable infrastructure" pattern,
continuous delivery, staged rollout cultures.

Use when:
- teams want speed but user trust, data integrity, or availability matters;
- AI/coding agents are about to change production-facing behavior.

Artifact:

```text
change:
speed need:
blast radius:
feature flag / staged rollout:
test gate:
monitor:
rollback:
owner:
```

Validation:
- Speed is paired with automated checks, rollout control, and rollback.
- "Fast" is not accepted as a reason to skip safety gates.

Do not use when:
- the current system lacks enough observability to detect harm.

### 15. Craft and Detail Culture

Lineage: Patrick Collison, Stripe-style developer experience, Jobs-style craft.

Use when:
- the output is a product surface, API, docs, CLI, onboarding, or artifact that
  users will repeatedly touch;
- small friction compounds into adoption loss.

Artifact:

```text
user journey:
first five minutes:
paper cuts:
delight/detail opportunities:
error language:
docs/API symmetry:
quality bar:
regression check:
```

Validation:
- The artifact is tested from a new user's first contact.
- Details improve comprehension or trust, not decoration.

Do not use when:
- polish would delay a critical safety or correctness fix.

### 16. Startup Execution Contract

Lineage: Sam Altman, YC startup playbooks, high-growth operator practice.

Use when:
- a new product, project, agent workflow, or repository initiative needs focus;
- teams are confusing ideas with execution.

Artifact:

```text
idea:
market/user:
team/capabilities:
product:
execution loop:
single most important metric:
weekly proof:
failure threshold:
```

Validation:
- Progress is tied to a weekly proof, not narrative optimism.
- Weakness in idea, team, product, or execution is named directly.

Do not use when:
- the task is research without a product or adoption path.

### 17. Software-Leverage Reframe

Lineage: Marc Andreessen, software-platform strategy, automation economics.

Use when:
- a domain may be transformed by turning repeated human coordination into
  software, APIs, data flows, or agents;
- a legacy workflow hides a software bottleneck.

Artifact:

```text
legacy value chain:
repeatable decision:
software representation:
data/control interface:
new feedback loop:
new failure mode:
human override:
```

Validation:
- The reframe identifies new risks as well as leverage.
- Human agency, privacy, and domain exceptions remain visible.

Do not use when:
- the domain's value is primarily embodied, relational, legal, or ethical and
  cannot be safely flattened into software.

### 18. Blitzscale Risk Gate

Lineage: Reid Hoffman, Silicon Valley scaleup practice.

Use only when:
- speed is a decisive advantage;
- network effects, marketplace liquidity, or winner-take-most dynamics exist;
- capital, hiring, support, and compliance runway can absorb inefficiency.

Artifact:

```text
speed thesis:
market structure:
network effect:
runway:
acceptable inefficiency:
trust/compliance guardrails:
stop condition:
```

Validation:
- The team can explain why slower, safer scaling would lose the market.
- Guardrails name customer trust, legal, support, and operational debt.

Do not use by default:
- Most projects should use stable-speed delivery, not blitzscaling.

### 19. Margin-of-Safety Thesis Gate

Lineage: Benjamin Graham, David Dodd, Warren Buffett, Charlie Munger,
Howard Marks.

Use when:
- an investment, acquisition, business, or capital-allocation thesis needs
  downside-first discipline;
- enthusiasm, narrative, or recent price action may be outrunning evidence.

Artifact:

```text
asset/business:
circle of competence:
thesis:
intrinsic-value range or value driver:
price / cost / commitment:
margin of safety:
downside and permanent-loss risk:
disconfirming evidence:
time horizon / catalyst:
position or exposure limit:
```

Validation:
- Downside, uncertainty, and disconfirming evidence are written before upside.
- The output is educational/process analysis unless a regulated advisory role
  and user-specific mandate are explicit.

Do not use when:
- the user asks for a quick buy/sell signal, price target, or guaranteed return.

### 20. Second-Level Market Expectations Ledger

Lineage: Howard Marks, George Soros, Eugene Fama, Kenneth French.

Use when:
- market prices may already reflect obvious facts;
- a thesis depends on being different from consensus and correct;
- reflexive feedback between beliefs, prices, and fundamentals may matter.

Artifact:

```text
observable fact:
consensus interpretation:
price-implied expectation:
variant perception:
why variant could be right:
why market could be right:
reflexive feedback loop:
evidence needed:
confidence:
```

Validation:
- The ledger distinguishes "different" from "better evidenced".
- Reflexivity is treated as a testable feedback path, not a catch-all story.

Do not use when:
- the task needs a neutral baseline or index/default answer rather than active
  market disagreement.

### 21. Portfolio Construction and Sizing Gate

Lineage: Harry Markowitz, William Sharpe, John Kelly, Ed Thorp, David Swensen,
Ray Dalio.

Use when:
- multiple bets, assets, projects, or risks must be combined;
- sizing, diversification, liquidity, drawdown, or leverage can dominate
  single-idea quality.

Artifact:

```text
objective:
constraints:
candidate exposures:
expected return uncertainty:
correlation / shared factor:
drawdown tolerance:
liquidity and time horizon:
position-size rule:
leverage / funding limit:
stress cases:
rebalance or stop rule:
```

Validation:
- Risk is measured by contribution to the whole portfolio, not only by
  standalone volatility or narrative comfort.
- Kelly-style sizing is capped or fractional unless edge and distribution are
  unusually well measured.

Do not use when:
- there is only one binary decision and no capital, capacity, or downside
  allocation problem.

### 22. Model Risk and Tail Scenario Gate

Lineage: Benoit Mandelbrot, Nassim Taleb, Fischer Black, Myron Scholes,
Robert Merton, risk-management practice.

Use when:
- a model, forecast, normal-distribution assumption, derivative, leverage, or
  backtest may understate extreme outcomes;
- ruin, liquidity failure, or nonlinear payoff matters.

Artifact:

```text
model:
core assumptions:
distribution / regime assumption:
tail exposure:
convexity / optionality:
liquidity and funding stress:
what breaks first:
hedge / reserve / kill trigger:
scenario tests:
residual risk:
```

Validation:
- At least one severe but plausible tail case is carried through the artifact.
- The model is useful only within named assumptions; outside them, the response
  switches to robustness and survival.

Do not use when:
- it would turn a simple nonfinancial task into speculative disaster analysis.

### 23. Systematic Signal Research Gate

Lineage: Jim Simons, Renaissance Technologies, Ed Thorp, Eugene Fama,
Kenneth French.

Use when:
- a quantitative or repeatable signal is proposed;
- data, transaction costs, leakage, overfitting, capacity, or regime decay
  could erase an apparent edge.

Artifact:

```text
signal hypothesis:
data source:
target / label:
leakage checks:
baseline:
transaction and slippage costs:
out-of-sample test:
capacity / crowding:
decay and regime risk:
deployment guardrail:
```

Validation:
- In-sample fit is never treated as evidence without out-of-sample or
  forward-test support.
- Costs, capacity, and leakage are checked before claiming edge.

Do not use when:
- the task lacks repeatable data or the user needs judgment under sparse facts.

### 24. Financial Engineering Replication Gate

Lineage: Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, Robert Merton, Black-Litterman,
derivatives and structured-finance practice.

Use when:
- a payoff, hedge, derivative, structured product, or synthetic exposure must
  be understood;
- the task needs replication, arbitrage bounds, Greeks, or confidence-weighted
  views.

Artifact:

```text
payoff:
underlying exposures:
replicating portfolio:
arbitrage bounds:
Greeks / sensitivities:
funding and liquidity assumptions:
hedge schedule:
view / prior / confidence:
stress result:
residual basis risk:
```

Validation:
- The same payoff is explained as cash flows, exposures, and failure modes.
- Assumptions about continuous trading, liquidity, funding, and volatility are
  explicit.

Do not use when:
- a plain-language risk explanation is enough and engineering detail would add
  false precision.

## Composition Rules

- For coding: Contract Gate + Gemba Kaizen + Stable-Speed Delivery.
- For research: Dialectic Refutation + Evidence Garden + Experiment Ledger.
- For hidden-rule discovery: Thought Experiment + Evidence Garden + Fog Loop.
- For product design: Customer-Backwards + Unscalable Truth Probe + Craft.
- For ambitious architecture: First-Principles Moonshot + Pattern Language +
  Contract Gate.
- For defensive security: Fog Loop + Dialectic Refutation + Stable-Speed
  Delivery.
- For narrative/UI expression: Narrative Resonance + Craft + Pattern Language.
- For investment or capital-allocation reasoning: Margin-of-Safety Thesis +
  Second-Level Expectations + Portfolio Construction.
- For quant or factor research: Systematic Signal Research + Model Risk +
  Portfolio Construction.
- For derivatives, hedging, or structured products: Financial Engineering
  Replication + Model Risk + Formal Contract.
